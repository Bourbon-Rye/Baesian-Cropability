Background

You picked up the latest edition of the newspaper

After the pandemic-driven recession in 2020, the island (i.e., the Philippines) is still experiencing economic and food crisis. Agriculture plays a huge role in the lives of Filipinos. However, our nation still struggles when it comes to food security. Shortage of food supply coming from the agricultural sector is common. Leading causes are rampant hoarding, inadequate government policies, and unforeseen natural crises. In 2023, to combat increasing price of rice, President Marcos declared price ceiling to rice products fueling economic insecurity of farmers. Subsequently, this policy adds pressure in producing food crops and in selling them in the market. In line with this, cases of vegetables, such as cabbages, being wasted or sold at a loss due to oversupply in the food supply chain increases. This is food that could be bought by the government and reallocated to starving families. With the ever increasing population growth and unpredictable crises, the capacity to produce food for the population is hampered. Sustainable agriculture and food production is needed now more than ever.

It is time to plan out on how you will organize your data

You establish the following steps to ensure the quality of the data.
The team gathered most of the required data from the database maintained by the Philippine Statistical Authority (PSA). Various datasets were collected with varying temporal (i.e. Period or Datetime) granularity and range (with a unified range of 1994-2023). However, due to granularity issues, only the annualized range 2012-2023 were used for hypothesis testing, but the entire 1994-2023 range is still used for machine learning as detailed later.

To autonomously gather the data from PSA, the team developed scrapers using Mechanical Soup.

Due to the limitations of the scraper, the team also developed a program to compile fragmented datasets into one with the help of Pandas.
You begin to sanitize and standardize the data you've collected:
For proper format consistency, Year and Months column were properly arranged in chronological order. Additionally, textcases for some elements were fixed.
Converted and validated all ".." and 0 elements to blank representations for easy NA representation.
Removed columns such as "Annual" and "Average" that can be derived from current data to reduce data clutter.
Region-Province relationships were derived and kept into a separate file, and multiple region name corrections had to be done (e.g. ARMM vs BARMM).
Aside from geolocation naming issues, bad and non-standard commodity names and period names (e.g. abbreviated months vs. full months) also had to be fixed.
Period values standardized to the range 1994-2023, either Yearly, Quarterly, or Monthly (Yearly is where most the datasets intersect, hence it is used for hypothesis testing). Reduced number of features however are used for machine learning, hence allowing 1994-2023 Quarterly and Monthly to be used.
The following cereal crops are the main focus of this study. Given are some fun facts about them.

Crops:
Rice - The country is the 8th largest rice producer in the world. However, rice is the Philippines' 3rd largest agricultural import, meaning our nation is still struggling with agricultural self-sufficiency. Corn - This is the second most important crop in the country. Sweet corn is a staple treat for Filipinos and its grains are also an important feed for livestock.

The following are the collected agricultural and socioeconomic indicators to be used as features.

Agricultural Indicators:
Stocks - Sum of household, commercial, and NFA commodity stocks./p2> Volume - Total quantity of a crop produced and harvested, including portions damaged, stolen, given away, consumed, or reserved. Area Harvested - Actual area from which crops are harvested, excluding totally damaged areas. Self-Sufficiency Ratio - Measures the extent to which a country's domestic production meets its domestic utilization. Import-Dependency Ratio - Measures the extent to which a country relies on imports to meet its domestic consumption needs for crops. SU Gross Supply - Total quantity of food available before any use, including beginning stocks, production, and imports. UT Consumable - Amount of food commodity available in its original (unprocessed) form for human consumption.
SU = Supply Utilization, UT = Utilization Total

Socioeconomic Indicators:
Farmgate Price - Price received by farmers for their crops (excludes transport and marketing costs). Retail Price - Price at which goods are sold to regular consumers. Wholesale Price - Price at which goods are sold in large quantities from producers or distributors to retailers. CPI All Income - Change in average retail prices of a fixed basket of goods and services purchased by all households compared to a base year CPI Bottom 30 - Change in average retail prices of a fixed basket of goods and services purchased by the lowest income 30% of households compared to a base year Net Returns - Commodity total production costs (cash and non-cash) minus total revenue from commodity Inflation Rate - Percentage change in the average price level of goods and services over a specific period, compared to the previous period or year NCR RPI - Food Retail Price Index at the National Capital Region
RPI = Retail Price Index, CPI = Consumer Price Index

Results

You were invited to talk in the council meeting today

This is the culmination of your research on this island. It is time to tell the isle-folks your findings and help them make an informed decision for their future. You started with discussing the insights you gained while exploring the data.
Result 1: The significant correlations and regional differences highlight that some market conditions, such as inflation rate, play a role in determining agricultural productivity. Generally, a low price on grains signifies a high self-sufficiency ratio for the agricultural sector.

Result 2: Despite having growing stocks on grains every year, prices still continue to grow. This can be attributed to the continuous growth of the population and increasing reliance on much more expensive imported grains.

Result 3: Policies attempting to stabilize rice and corn supplies only work for a short period of time. This highlights the weakness of the island to find solutions for sustainable agriculture and a stable market.

With this information, you think you can help the people realize the importance of the study by showing the prediction of the future of their agriculture.
Your EDA has given you more confidence that more patterns in your data can be learned. That is, there are more nuances embedded in the data than meets the (statistical) eye.

During this portion of your presentation, you showcase your ML experiments from start to finish to supplement your EDA findings and to better show the use of ML models for time-series analysis and forecasting (e.g. in predicting how the island's agriculture might perform in the near future). "Give a man a bowl of rice and he eats for a day, give a man a love for data science and he can model agricultural trends to get plenty more rice."

You focus your analysis on rice and its two agricultural indicators that are most important for the people: volume of rice production and rice stocks.

By preprocessing data and getting exogenous variables (e.g. the market indicators) that you deemed helpful in your model's prediction, you are now prepared to split the data into training and testing sets.

To actually find out which regression model suits best with the given data, you analyze their performance or the quality of their predictions using Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) and the Coefficient of Determination (R2). MAPE is to minimized while R2 is to be maximized while performing other validation to avoid overfitting.

After optimization and various experimentations on different models and parameter sets with respect to MAPE and R2, you determine that Elastic Net CV is the best model for predicting the (quarterly) volume of rice production and that Random Forest Regressor is the best model for predicting (monthly) rice stocks. You found that the volume of rice production on the island would likely stay the same in the near future, while the rice stocks on the island, despite dipping for a short period, can eventually rebound. You share the news of better days ahead to the celebration of the islanders. The anomaly is in fact not an anomaly, but a pattern to be understood. Through your experiments, you also found out that various combinations of the market indicators have very minimal impact on the quality of the predictions, hence you are more keen to acknowledge that the answer to your primary research question is that the tested market indicators are only weakly correlated to agricultural productivity.

The people thanks you for giving them these models as these will help them people make an informed decision about future policies and redirect their prospects towards a more sustainable agriculture.

This led you to conclude that...

Economic factors only weakly affect food crop productivity, but government policies such as price ceilings and revised tarrifs, as well as climate phenomenona such as El Nio evidently affect food crop productivity as seen through time series analysis. This highlights towards the potential role of market conditions and more explicitly government policies in regulating agricultural productivity.

With this conclusion in mind, you connected it to the following implications for the future of cereal crops in the island.
This overall highlights that the island is not self-sufficient in terms of both rice and corn production. Analysis suggests that being self-sufficient is a key to lower domestic prices on food crops and can stabilize market conditions.

In relation to this, the island is currently experiencing inflation on rice prices. This can be attributed to the fact that the local production is low and expensive sanctions on cereal crops imports. Furthermore, having to rely on imports also introduces competition to domestic producers which harms the ability of the island to truly sustain itself.

All this trickles down to the realization that the current laws and policies of the government towards food sustainability is not enough. Analysis shows that food crops are affected by some market factors and policies currently imposed. With continuous population growth, policymakers should consider focusing on strategies pertaining to sustainable agriculture and market stability.
